📘 About This Calculator

This calculator determines the Design Quantity of CO₂ (mDQ) needed for local application fire suppression systems. It uses the Rate of Discharge (W), Discharge Time (t), and High Pressure Efficiency Factor (E) to calculate the total CO₂ required. This ensures sufficient gas is delivered for effective fire suppression while optimizing system efficiency and safety compliance.

⚙️ Parameters Used

  • Rate of Discharge (W): CO₂ released per second (kg/s).
  • Discharge Time (t): Duration of CO₂ release (seconds).
  • High Pressure Efficiency Factor (E): Correction factor for system efficiency.

📌 Input Validation Rules

  • ✅ All fields mandatory: Rate of Discharge (W), Discharge Time (t), and Efficiency Factor (E) must be filled.
  • ✅ Numeric values only: Letters, symbols, or empty inputs are invalid.
  • ✅ Positive values required: W, t, and E must be greater than zero.
  • ✅ Practical ranges: W: 0.1–500 kg/s, t: 5–300 s, E: 0.7–1.0 (per NFPA/ISO guidelines).
  • ✅ Decimal precision: W and t max 2 decimals, E max 4 decimals.
  • ⚠️ Edge cases: Extremely low/high inputs or unrealistic combinations trigger warnings. mDQ < 50 kg or > 50,000 kg alerts user.

📐 Governing Principle

For local application CO₂ fire suppression systems, the industry standard is NFPA 12, Standard on Carbon Dioxide Extinguishing Systems. This standard dictates the requirements for designing, installing, and maintaining the system, including the calculation of discharge rates.

Other standards are also referenced for CO₂ systems:

  • Indian Standard (IS) 15528: This is an Indian standard for gaseous fire extinguishing systems.
  • British Standard (BS) 5306: This British standard also applies to CO₂ fire extinguishing systems.
  • ISO 6183: This ISO standard provides requirements and recommendations for fixed carbon dioxide firefighting systems.

Key aspects of local application discharge rate calculation under NFPA 12:

  • A local application system discharges CO₂ directly onto the fire, rather than flooding an entire enclosed space.
  • The calculation is based on the protected surface area, not the volume of the room.
  • The system uses a “rate-by-area” method to determine the required flow rate.
  • Nozzles are spaced according to the manufacturer’s rated coverage.
  • The total system flow rate is found by multiplying the number of nozzles by the flow rate per nozzle.
  • The minimum discharge time for local application is 30 seconds.
  • The calculations also factor in a percentage increase to compensate for the vapor portion of the discharge and to ensure sufficient agent is discharged.

The Discharge Rate of CO₂ for local applications is governed by NFPA 12, which provides guidance for systems that protect only part of a hazard. It defines the required CO₂ quantity, nozzle discharge characteristics, and safety measures to ensure effective fire suppression while protecting personnel and equipment. Following these standards ensures that local-application CO₂ systems operate safely and efficiently.
