📘 About This Calculator

The Condensate Velocity Calculator determines the velocity of condensate in a pipeline, ensuring efficient steam system operation and proper pipe sizing to prevent issues like erosion, water hammer, and pressure drop.

❓ What is Condensate Velocity?

Condensate velocity is the speed at which condensate flows through a pipe. It's a critical parameter in steam systems where proper velocity ensures efficient operation while preventing damage to piping components.

⚙️ Parameters Used

  • Condensate Flowrate: The mass flow of condensate through the pipe, measured in kg/h.
  • Specific Volume: The volume occupied by each kg of condensate, measured in m³/kg.
  • Pipe Bore: The internal diameter of the pipe carrying condensate, measured in mm.

📊 Interpretation

  • Below 5 m/s → Low velocity: May indicate oversized piping or low flowrate
  • 5 – 30 m/s → Optimal velocity: Within recommended range for efficient condensate return
  • Above 30 m/s → High velocity: Risk of pipe erosion, noise, and water hammer

💡 Important Note

This calculation is based on standard fluid dynamics principles. Real-world performance may vary due to pipe roughness, temperature variations, and system configuration. Always incorporate appropriate safety factors in engineering designs.

🌐 Where is this Calculation Used?

  • Steam & Condensate Recovery Systems: Ensuring proper flow in return lines.
  • HVAC & Process Heating: Managing condensate movement in heating applications.
  • Industrial Steam Systems: Preventing erosion, water hammer, and pipe damage.

✅ Input Validation Rules (Compliance-Based)

  • Condensate Flowrate: 1 – 50,000 kg/h (≤3 decimals)
  • Specific Volume: 0.0001 – 0.1 m³/kg (≤4 decimals)
  • Pipe Bore: 10 – 500 mm (≤1 decimal)
  • Output Velocity:
    • <0.1 m/s → Invalid (likely input error)
    • 0.1 – 5 m/s → Low (inefficient, but acceptable)
    • 5 – 30 m/s → Optimal (industry standard range)
    • 30 – 100 m/s → High (erosion/water hammer risk)
    • >100 m/s → Unrealistic (check inputs)

⚠️ These thresholds align with ISO 45001, OSHA, NFPA, and ILO safety guidelines for steam/condensate piping design. Always include safety factors in engineering reports.
