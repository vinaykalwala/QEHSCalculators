📘 About the Safety Index

The **Safety Index (SI)** is a type of benefit-cost ratio used to evaluate the economic viability of a safety project. It quantifies the financial return on an investment by comparing the projected monetary savings from prevented accidents to the total cost of implementing the safety measure. A higher SI value indicates a more cost-effective project.

⚙️ Formula Used

First, the total projected savings (G) are calculated:

$$ G = D \times E \times \left(\frac{F}{100}\right) $$

Then, the Safety Index (SI) is calculated as the ratio of savings to cost:

$$ \text{SI} = \frac{\text{Total Savings (G)}}{\text{Total Project Cost (C)}} $$

  • $D$ = Number of Accidents Prevented
  • $E$ = Cost per Accident
  • $F$ = Effectiveness Factor (%)

💡 Interpreting the SI

The SI value provides a clear directive for decision-making:

  • SI > 1.0: The project's financial benefits (savings from prevented accidents) outweigh its costs. It is considered economically justifiable.
  • SI = 1.0: The benefits are equal to the costs. The project breaks even.
  • SI < 1.0: The project's costs are greater than its financial benefits. While it may still have non-monetary value, it is not considered economically efficient.
