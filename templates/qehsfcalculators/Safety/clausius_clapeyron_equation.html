📘 About This Calculator

This tool calculates vapor pressure at different temperatures using the Clausius-Clapeyron Equation, which relates vapor pressure to temperature for phase changes. It's essential for predicting how pressure changes with temperature during evaporation/condensation.

⚙️ Parameters Used

  • Initial Pressure (P₁): Vapor pressure at the initial temperature (atm).
  • Initial Temperature (T₁): Absolute temperature in Kelvin (K).
  • Final Pressure (P₂): Vapor pressure at the final temperature (atm). Leave blank to calculate.
  • Final Temperature (T₂): Absolute temperature in Kelvin (K). Leave blank to calculate.
  • Enthalpy of Vaporization (ΔHvap): Energy needed for liquid→gas transition (kJ/mol).

📊 Interpretation

Pressure Safety Guidelines:

  • Below 0.1 atm → Low pressure - minimal vapor risk
  • 0.1-1 atm → Moderate pressure - safe for standard use
  • 1-10 atm → High pressure - pressure-rated containers required
  • Above 10 atm → Extreme pressure - serious explosion risk

🔑 Key Applications

  • Chemical Storage: Predicting pressure buildup in sealed containers.
  • Fire Hazard Assessment: Estimating flammable vapor concentrations.
  • Process Safety: Determining safe operating temperature ranges.
  • Spill Behavior: Calculating evaporation rates of hazardous liquids.

✅ Validation Rules (Safety-Critical)

  • P₁ (Initial Pressure): 0.0001 – 200 atm
  • T₁ (Initial Temperature): 50 – 2000 K
  • P₂ (Final Pressure): 0.0001 – 200 atm (only if T₂ blank)
  • T₂ (Final Temperature): 50 – 2000 K (only if P₂ blank)
  • ΔHvap: 5 – 100 kJ/mol (typical liquids, e.g., water = 40.7)
  • Cross-field: Only one of P₂ or T₂ must be entered
  • Outputs: Must be positive, finite, and within industrial safety ranges

⚠️ Why Only One of P₂ or T₂?

  • The Clausius-Clapeyron equation is designed to solve for the missing value when you already know three parameters.
  • If you provide both P₂ and T₂, there's nothing left to calculate.
  • To calculate effectively, enter either P₂ or T₂, and the equation will find the other.

🏭 Industry Standards & Guidelines

OSHA Process Safety Management (PSM) and EPA Risk Management Program (RMP) require vapor pressure calculations. NFPA 30 uses this for flammable liquid classification.
