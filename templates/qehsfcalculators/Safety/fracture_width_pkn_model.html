‚öñ Governing Principle

The Perkins-Kern-Nordgren (PKN) model for hydraulic fracture width is a foundational engineering model developed by Perkins, Kern, and Nordgren in the 1960s and 1970s. It is widely used in the oil and gas industry but does not adhere to a single, codified standard like ISO or API. Instead, it serves as a well-established theoretical framework and standard practice for analyzing and predicting hydraulic fracture behavior.

Key Aspects of the PKN Model:

  • Theoretical Basis: A 2D model assuming vertical, plane-strain conditions within a fixed-height, elliptical cross-section fracture.
  • Assumptions: Applicable when fracture length ‚â´ height and includes the effect of fluid leak-off into the formation.
  • Development: Originated from Perkins and Kern (1961) and refined by Nordgren (1972) to include fluid-loss effects.
  • Applications: Commonly used in hydraulic fracturing for predicting fracture geometry and width when fracture height is limited and fracture length is dominant.

The PKN model remains a cornerstone in hydraulic fracturing design, balancing simplicity with accuracy for most low-to-moderate height fracture scenarios.
