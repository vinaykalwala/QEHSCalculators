📘 About This Calculator

This tool calculates the volume of a hydraulic fracture using the PKN (Perkins–Kern–Nordgren) model. It helps engineers design and monitor hydraulic fracturing operations in oil, gas, and geothermal applications to optimize efficiency while maintaining safety and environmental compliance.

⚙️ Parameters Used

  • Fracture Half-Length (xf): Distance from the fracture center to its tip (ft).
  • Fracture Height (hf): Vertical height of the fracture (ft).
  • Average Fracture Width (w̄m): Mean width of the fracture (ft).
  • Fracture Volume (Vf): Total volume of the hydraulic fracture (ft³).

✅ Input Validation Rules

  • Fracture Half-Length (xf): 10–2000 ft, must be > 0, up to 2 decimals allowed.
  • Fracture Height (hf): 10–500 ft, must be > 0, up to 2 decimals allowed.
  • Average Fracture Width (w̄m): 0.01–0.5 ft, must be > 0, up to 3 decimals allowed.
  • Error Prevention: Negative, zero, empty, and unrealistic values are automatically blocked.

📊 Output Validations

Typical Output Range: 1–100,000 ft³. Values outside this range trigger a warning.

  • Fracture Volume (Vf) Below 100 ft³ → Small fracture volume.
  • Fracture Volume (Vf) 100–1,000 ft³ → Moderate fracture volume.
  • Fracture Volume (Vf) 1,000–10,000 ft³ → Medium fracture volume.
  • Fracture Volume (Vf) 10,000–50,000 ft³ → Large fracture volume.
  • Fracture Volume (Vf) Above 50,000 ft³ → Very large fracture volume.

Assessment: The system provides real-time feedback such as “Small fracture volume,” “Moderate fracture volume,” or “Very large fracture volume — check regulatory compliance.”

🧭 Governing Principle

The PKN (Perkins–Kern–Nordgren) model for calculating fracture volume isn't tied to a single, universally recognized standard like a physical code or regulation, but rather it is a well-established, classic analytical model within the oil and gas industry for hydraulic fracturing. It represents a fundamental approach to modeling fracture geometry and volume, often serving as a basis for more complex models and simulations.

Key Aspects of the PKN Model:

  • Foundation: Based on the work of Perkins and Kern (1961), with later contributions from Nordgren.
  • Assumptions: Constant fracture height, elliptical cross-section, and plane strain conditions.
  • Industry Use: Widely used as a benchmark and educational model in hydraulic fracturing studies.
  • Basis for Other Models: Serves as a foundation for pseudo-3D (P3D) and 3D models.
  • Analytical Tool: Offers analytical expressions for fracture width, volume, and length estimation.

In essence, the PKN model is considered a theoretical standard widely adopted across the industry, forming the basis of hydraulic fracture design and analysis.

✅ Conclusion

Using this calculation, engineers can design effective and controlled fractures to maximize hydrocarbon or heat extraction while ensuring environmental protection, structural safety, and regulatory compliance. The model also provides insights into fracture growth behavior for efficient reservoir stimulation planning.
