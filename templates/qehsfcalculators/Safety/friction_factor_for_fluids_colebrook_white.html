📘 About This Calculator

The Colebrook-White equation calculates the friction factor (f) for turbulent fluid flow in pipes. It's essential in hydraulic and fluid mechanics to determine pressure loss due to pipe friction.

⚙️ Parameters Used

  • Absolute Pipe Roughness (kₛ): The roughness height of the pipe's inner surface (m).
  • Pipe Bore (D): The internal diameter of the pipe (m).
  • Reynolds Number (Re): A dimensionless quantity representing the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces in the flow.

📊 Explanation of Parameters

Parameter details and their significance:

  • Absolute Pipe Roughness (kₛ): Varies by material (e.g., 0.0015mm for plastic, 0.045mm for commercial steel)
  • Pipe Bore (D): Larger diameters generally result in lower friction factors
  • Reynolds Number (Re): Values > 4000 indicate turbulent flow where this equation applies

📈 Interpretation

Friction factor values indicate flow resistance. Generally:

  • Below 0.015 → Low friction, smooth pipe
  • 0.015 – 0.03 → Moderate friction, typical for engineering applications
  • Above 0.03 → High friction, significant resistance requiring attention

🔍 Real-life Applications

The Colebrook-White equation is crucial for:

  • Pipelines & Water Supply Systems: Designing efficient water distribution networks
  • HVAC Systems: Determining pressure losses in air ducts and ventilation systems
  • Oil & Gas Industry: Calculating frictional losses in pipelines
  • Fire Protection Systems: Ensuring proper flow rate and pressure for firefighting systems
  • Hydraulic Engineering: Designing canals, tunnels, and closed conduits

✅ Input Validation Rules

  • Absolute Pipe Roughness (kₛ): Must be between 0 and 0.01 m.
  • Pipe Bore (D): Must be between 0.001 m and 10 m.
  • Reynolds Number (Rₑ): Must be between 4000 and 1×10⁸ (turbulent flow only).
  • Relative Roughness (kₛ/D): Must be ≤ 0.05.
  • Cross-field check: kₛ must always be smaller than D.
  • Output Validity: Friction factor (f) must converge and lie between 0.007 and 0.08.

Note: These rules follow ASME, ISO 5167, NFPA, and hydraulic engineering standards.
