📘 About This Calculator

The Total Recordable Case Frequency (TRCF) is a lagging indicator in occupational health and safety. It measures the number of recordable incidents (lost time injuries, restricted work, medical treatment cases) relative to total hours worked, standardizing safety performance for comparison across time periods and industries.

⚙️ Parameters Used

  • Total Recordable Cases (TRC): Number of incidents requiring recordable reporting.
  • Total Hours Worked: Total employee work hours in the period considered.
  • Formula: TRCF = (Total Recordable Cases × 1,000,000) / Total Hours Worked

📝 Input Validation Rules

  • Total Recordable Cases (TRC): Whole number ≥ 0. Fractional or negative numbers are invalid.
  • Total Hours Worked: Positive number > 0. Zero or negative values are invalid.
  • Cross-field consistency: Extremely high TRC relative to hours (e.g., TRC > hours/100) → ⚠️ Warning: Verify realism.
  • Blank, non-numeric, negative, or fractional TRC → flagged as invalid.
  • Blank, non-numeric, or ≤0 Total Hours Worked → flagged as invalid.

📊 Output Validations

  • TRCF displayed with 2 decimal places.
  • If calculation produces NaN or infinite → ❌ "Calculation resulted in an invalid number. Please check your inputs."
  • Performance Assessment:
    • ✅ Excellent Performance: TRCF ≤ 1.0 – Strong safety culture, world-class performance.
    • 👍 Good Performance: TRCF ≤ 2.5 – Healthy range, maintain proactive safety measures.
    • 🟡 Moderate Performance: TRCF ≤ 4.0 – Room for improvement, review incident trends.
    • ❌ High Frequency: TRCF > 4.0 – Elevated incidents, immediate review of safety management recommended.

📏 Governing Principle

A Total Recordable Case Frequency (TRCF) Calculator is not based on a single global standard but is a calculation derived from the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) recording criteria in the United States, and is also used by various international safety organizations like BIMCO for maritime safety. OSHA's definition of what constitutes a recordable case—death, days away from work, restricted work or job transfer, or medical treatment beyond first aid—forms the basis for the TRCF or TRIR (Total Recordable Incident Rate) calculation.

  • OSHA's Role: Defines recordable injuries/illnesses. Cases include death, days away from work, job transfer, restricted work, or medical treatment beyond first aid.
  • International Use: TRCF is also used internationally (e.g., BIMCO) for consistent safety measurement. Companies use TRCF/TRIR for internal benchmarking and performance tracking.
  • Purpose: Tool to measure workplace injuries and illnesses, track trends, and improve safety performance.

✅ Conclusion

Using a TRCF calculator enables organizations to quantify safety performance, identify trends, and benchmark against industry standards. Proper input validation ensures accurate calculations and actionable insights to maintain or improve workplace safety.
