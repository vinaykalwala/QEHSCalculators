📘 About This Calculator

The Pitot Tube Velocity Calculator determines the instantaneous fluid velocity in a duct, pipe, or stack by measuring the pressure difference between stagnation and static pressure. It is widely used for ventilation, environmental monitoring, and industrial safety audits.

⚙ Parameters Used

  • ΔP (Pressure Difference): Pa — measured between stagnation and static pressure
  • ρ (Fluid Density): kg/m³ — density of the flowing fluid
  • Cₚ (Pitot Coefficient): dimensionless — typically 0.95–1.0, accounts for losses and calibration

✅ Input Validation Rules

  • ΔP must be numeric and ≥ 0 Pa
  • ρ must be numeric and > 0 kg/m³
  • Cₚ must be numeric and between 0.95 and 1.0
  • Zero, negative, or non-numeric entries are invalid
  • Warnings for very low ΔP (<0.1 Pa), unrealistic density (<0.1 or >5000 kg/m³), or low Cₚ (<0.98)

📊 Output Validations

  • Velocity displayed in m/s to 4 decimal places
  • Assessment/feedback displayed along with result:
    • V (Velocity) < 1 → ⚠️ Very low airflow - check for blockages or errors
    • 1 ≤ V (Velocity) ≤ 20 → ✅ Normal operational velocity
    • 20 < V (Velocity) ≤ 50 → ⚠️ High velocity - verify duct design and safety
    • V (Velocity) > 50 → 🔴 Extremely high velocity - likely measurement error
  • Input values ΔP, ρ, Cₚ displayed alongside velocity
  • Warnings for unusual inputs included

📐 Governing Principle

A pitot tube velocity calculator is based on established engineering standards, primarily ISO 3966 (Measurement of fluid flow in closed conduits — Velocity area method using Pitot static tubes) and national standards such as the Indian Standard IS 14973. These standards provide methods for measuring fluid flow and velocity using pitot tubes, detailing the technology, calculations, and conditions for applying the velocity-area method for accurate results in closed conduits.

Key Standards and Their Focus:

  • ISO 3966: International standard specifying methods for Pitot tube measurements, calculating local velocities from differential pressures, applicable to fluids of constant density, low Mach numbers (<0.25), and steady flow.
  • IS 14973 (2001): Indian Standard adaptation of the velocity-area method using pitot tubes for closed conduits.
  • EPA Method 2: U.S. Environmental Protection Agency procedure for stack gas velocity and volumetric flow rate using standard pitot tubes, referencing calibration standards such as NIST.

How Calculators Use These Standards:

  • Underlying Principle: Measures difference between stagnation and static pressure to determine velocity.
  • Formulas: Derived from Bernoulli’s principle, incorporating a velocity coefficient (Cₚ) through calibration.
  • Application: Ensures accurate velocity measurements for design, monitoring, and safety verification in various systems.

🔎 Conclusion

This calculator provides fluid velocity for given ΔP, ρ, and Cₚ inputs, displaying results along with assessment/feedback and warnings for extreme values. It assists engineers and environmental professionals in evaluating airflow, verifying duct or stack design, and ensuring compliance with industrial safety standards.
