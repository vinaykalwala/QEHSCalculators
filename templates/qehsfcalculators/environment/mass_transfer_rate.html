📘 About This Calculator

This calculator estimates the steady-state mass transfer rate for gas-to-liquid transfer under a film-controlled regime. Use consistent units: KL in m/s, a in m²/m³, concentrations in mg/L. The result is given in mg/(L·s).

⚙ Parameters Used

  • KL: Liquid-side mass transfer coefficient (m/s)
  • a: Interfacial area per unit volume (m²/m³)
  • Cs: Saturation concentration (mg/L)
  • C: Bulk concentration (mg/L)
  • ΔC = (Cs − C): Driving force (mg/L)

✅ Input Validation Rules

  • KL: numeric, >0, typical 1e-6 – 1e-2; errors if ≤0 or <1e-10 or >1.0
  • a: numeric, >0, typical 0.1 – 1e5; errors if ≤0 or <1e-6 or >1e6
  • Cs: numeric, >0, errors if ≤0 or >1e6
  • C: numeric, ≥0 and < Cs, errors if negative or ≥ Cs
  • Precision: >6 decimal places triggers a warning
  • Computation safety: results >1e6 mg/(L·s) treated as invalid

📊 Output Validations & Assessment

  • Computed mass transfer rate must be finite and > 0
  • Warnings for extreme values:
    • KL < 1e-6 → very slow transfer, check units
    • KL > 1e-1 → unusually high, verify source
    • a < 0.01 → low interfacial area, transfer may be limited
    • a > 1e5 → very large, check geometry and units
    • ΔC ≈ 0 → negligible driving force
    • Mass transfer rate > 1000 mg/(L·s) → verify inputs
    • Mass transfer rate < 1e-8 → below measurement sensitivity
  • Assessment / feedback displayed along with results:
    • Rate < 1 mg/(L·s) → Low → Consider increasing interfacial area or improving agitation/aeration
    • 1–50 mg/(L·s) → Moderate → Typical for many systems; validate with pilot data
    • >50 mg/(L·s) → High → Ensure process control and review mass balance

📖 Governing Principle

A mass transfer rate calculator is not of one single standard; rather, it is based on fundamental principles like Fick's Law of Diffusion for molecular mass transfer and various established theoretical and empirical correlations developed for different applications and equipment types. Standards like the ASCE Standard 2–06 provide guidelines for specific mass transfer contexts, but the calculations themselves rely on diverse methodologies depending on the specific process.

  • Fick's Law of Diffusion: Describes molecular transfer from high to low concentration due to a concentration gradient.
  • Theoretical Models: Predict mass transfer coefficients using dimensionless numbers (Reynolds, Sherwood, Schmidt) to account for physical properties and flow.
  • Application-Specific Standards: ASCE Standard 2–06 for gas transfer in water; BioProcess International uses kLa correlations for bioreactors.
  • Key Considerations: Process and equipment specificity, theoretical vs. empirical methods depending on available data.

Conclusion

  • Steady-state assumption — not valid for transient processes
  • Empirical correlations may vary depending on equipment and conditions
  • Results are generic — always compare with process-specific standards or experimental data
