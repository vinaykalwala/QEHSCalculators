Assessment & Notes

📘 About

This calculator estimates the particle diameter that is collected with 50% efficiency (dₚc) for a cyclone using the input operating and material parameters. Use consistent SI units for meaningful results.

⚙️ Applications of Cyclone Collection Efficiency

  • Used in industries to separate dust and particulate matter from flue gases.
  • Helps in controlling air pollution by removing suspended particles.
  • Widely applied in cement, steel, and chemical plants for dust control.

✅ Input Validation

  • Dynamic Viscosity (μ): Must be > 0. Typical gas range: 1×10⁻⁷ – 1×10⁻² kg·m⁻¹·s⁻¹.
  • Inlet Width (W): Must be > 0. Plausible range: 0.001 – 5 m.
  • Effective Turns (Nₑ): Must be > 0 (dimensionless). Values < 0.1 are unrealistic.
  • Inlet Velocity (Vᵢ): Must be > 0. Practical range: 0.1 – 200 m/s.
  • Particle Density (ρₚ): Must be > 0 and greater than gas density. Typical range: 10 – 20,000 kg/m³.
  • Gas Density (ρg): Must be > 0. Typically < 500 kg/m³ for most gases.
  • Cross-check: Ensure ρₚ > ρg (otherwise separation is physically impossible).
  • Units: Inputs must be entered in SI units as specified (kg·m⁻¹·s⁻¹, m, m/s, kg/m³).
  • Outliers: Warn if results yield dₚc < 0.1 μm or dₚc > 20,000 μm, as these are unrealistic in cyclone practice.

🔎 Interpretation

  • dₚc < 5 μm: cyclone captures very fine particles (high collection for fine dust).
  • 5–20 μm: moderate capture — typical performance for many cyclones.
  • > 20 μm: cyclone mostly captures coarse particles; poor for fine PM.
  • If the result seems unrealistic, re-check units (μ in kg/m·s, W in m, Vi in m/s, densities in kg/m³).
