📘 About This Calculator

The Cyclone Collection Efficiency Calculator estimates the efficiency with which a cyclone separator removes particles of a specific size, based on the relationship between the particle diameter and the cut-off diameter.

⚙ Parameters Used

  • Particle Diameter (dₚ): Micrometers (μm), typical range: 0.1–100 μm
  • Cut-off Diameter (dₚc): Micrometers (μm), typical range: 0.5–10 μm
  • Collection Efficiency (η): Percentage (0–100%)
  • Ratio (dₚ/dₚc): Dimensionless parameter influencing efficiency

✅ Input Validation Rules

  • 👉 Particle Diameter (dₚ) is required and must be > 0 μm
  • 👉 Cut-off Diameter (dₚc) is required and must be > 0 μm
  • ⚠️ Particle Diameter > 100 μm → unusually large (may settle before entry)
  • ⚠️ Cut-off Diameter > 20 μm → may indicate poor cyclone design
  • ⚠️ Ratio (dₚ/dₚc) < 0.1 → very low efficiency expected
  • ⚠️ Ratio (dₚ/dₚc) > 10 → efficiency approaches 100%
  • ❌ Non-numeric, zero, or negative inputs are invalid

📤 Output Validations

  • Efficiency (%) must be between 0 and 100
  • If computed efficiency is outside 0–100 → invalid result
  • Collection Efficiency ≥ 90% → ✅ Excellent collection efficiency (Highly effective cyclone performance)
  • 70% ≤ Collection Efficiency < 90% → ⚠️ Good collection efficiency (Adequate for most applications)
  • Collection Efficiency < 70% → 🔴 Poor collection efficiency (Consider cyclone improvements or alternative technology)

📐 Governing Principle

A cyclone collection particle removal efficiency calculator is based on the principles of empirical models, particularly the one developed by Lapple (1951), which relates particle size to collection efficiency. This approach, refined by Leith and Licht (1972) and others, uses parameters like the 50% cut-off particle size to predict the performance of a cyclone separator across various particle sizes.

📝 Conclusion

The cyclone efficiency calculator provides a reliable estimate of particle collection performance using the ratio of particle diameter to cut-off diameter. While the theoretical efficiency is helpful for design and evaluation, real-world performance may differ due to particle density, shape, and gas flow conditions.
