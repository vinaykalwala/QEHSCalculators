Assessment & Suggestions

📘 About This Calculator

It screens whether the created/propped fracture provides sufficient flow capacity relative to the reservoir matrix.

⚙ Parameters Used

  • kf (md): Fracture permeability.
  • wf (ft): Fracture width.
  • ke (md): Effective formation permeability.
  • xf (ft): Fracture half-length.

✅ Input Validation Rules

  • kf (md): Required, finite, 0.001–1×106. Typical proppant packs 10–100,000 md.
  • wf (ft): Required, finite, 0.0001–1. Typical created width 0.001–0.1 ft.
  • ke (md): Required, finite, 1×10−6–10,000. Typical 0.001–1,000 md.
  • xf (ft): Required, finite, 0.1–10,000. Typical 50–2,000 ft.
  • Cross-checks: Enforce wf < xf (geometric realism); warn if wf/xf > 5%.
  • Output sanity: FCD must be finite and in (0, 1×106]; warn if > 1,000 (possible overdesign/units mix).

📏 Output Validations

  • Fracture Conductivity (FCD): Displayed dimensionless with 4 decimals.
  • Assessment:
    • FCD < 1: Under-conductive — Increase width/proppant quality or half-length; evaluate kf damage and cleanup.
    • 1 ≤ FCD < 10: Adequate–Good — Likely sufficient conductivity for many reservoirs; confirm with rate/pressure data.
    • 10 ≤ FCD < 50: Strong — Diminishing returns likely; optimize proppant and pumping schedule.
    • FCD ≥ 50: Very High — Check economics and units; verify long-term pack stability and non-Darcy effects.
  • Warnings are triggered for extreme conditions: high FCD, unusual wf/xf, or low kf relative to ke.

📖 Governing Principle

The fracture conductivity calculator is based on the dimensionless fracture conductivity (FCD), defined as FCD = (kf·wf) / (ke·xf), where kf is fracture permeability, wf is fracture width, ke is effective formation permeability, and xf is fracture half-length. This parameter, rooted in Darcy's law, quantifies the flow capacity of a propped hydraulic fracture relative to the reservoir matrix, helping to assess whether the fracture provides sufficient conductivity for optimal production.

Key Concepts:

Darcy's Law Basis: The flow capacity of the fracture (kf·wf) is compared to the reservoir’s flow capacity (ke·xf), with FCD being dimensionless due to consistent units (md·ft / md·ft).

Purpose: A higher FCD indicates better fracture conductivity, but values above 50 may suggest overdesign, while values below 1 indicate insufficient conductivity.

Practical Application: Used to optimize fracture design, proppant selection, and pumping schedules, ensuring economic and safe production while avoiding issues like proppant crushing or excessive pressure.

🔚 Conclusion

This calculator provides reliable screening of fracture conductivity (FCD) to assess flow capacity relative to the reservoir matrix, supporting hydraulic fracturing design and production optimization. Ensure input values (kf, wf, ke, xf) are realistic and verified with field data to achieve accurate results and ensure well integrity and QEHS compliance.
