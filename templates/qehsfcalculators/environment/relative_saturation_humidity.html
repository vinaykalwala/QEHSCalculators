📘 About This Calculator

This calculator determines Relative Saturation (Relative Humidity, %) from measured vapor pressures. It helps identify risks of condensation, corrosion, mould, static electricity, and poor indoor air quality. Designed for use in QEHS, HVAC, fire safety, and environmental monitoring.

⚙ Parameters Used

  • pₐ (Pa): Actual vapor pressure of water in air.
  • Pₐ (Pa): Saturation vapor pressure at the same temperature.

✅ Input Validation Rules

  • pₐ: Required, positive, finite, in Pascals; < 1 Pa or > 200,000 Pa triggers warning.
  • Pₐ: Required, positive, finite, in Pascals; < 50 Pa or > 200,000 Pa triggers warning.
  • Constraints: pₐ cannot exceed Pₐ (physically impossible at same temperature).
  • Advisories: Warnings for suspiciously small or large pressures to check for unit or sensor errors.

📏 Output Validations

  • Relative Humidity (RH): Displayed as percentage with 2 decimals.
  • Assessment:
    • RH ≥ 99.5%: Saturated / Condensation likely — Immediate action: dehumidify, inspect, and protect assets.
    • 80% ≤ RH < 99.5%: High humidity — elevated risk — Monitor, inspect, and consider dehumidification.
    • 60% ≤ RH < 80%: Elevated humidity — watch — Maintain control, inspect equipment, review HVAC setpoints.
    • 30% ≤ RH < 60%: Comfortable / typical operating range — Maintain routine monitoring and preventive maintenance.
    • 20% ≤ RH < 30%: Low humidity — dry environment — Consider humidification, assess static risk.
    • RH < 20%: Very low humidity — high static risk — Immediate action: humidify, apply static-control measures.
  • Warnings triggered for: RH > 100% (clamped to 100%), RH < 20% (static/dust issues), 75% ≤ RH < 99.5% (moisture control needed), or invalid results (NaN, negative).

📖 Governing Principle

A relative humidity calculator uses formulas based on a specific standard or reference equation, with widely used examples including the Sonntag equation, which is specified in standards like British Standard 1339:1, and the Hyland-Wexler formula, used for specific temperature ranges. There isn't one universal "standard" for all calculators, as different formulas exist and are chosen for their accuracy and applicability to different conditions.

Factors determining a calculator's standard:

Formula Choice: The formula used to calculate water vapor pressure is the basis for humidity calculations, and different formulas (like Sonntag, Hyland-Wexler, or others) are used.

Temperature Range: The applicable temperature range of the formula is crucial, as some formulas are designed for broader or narrower temperatures.

Application: Specialized standards, such as ISO 18453 and IGT Bulletin #8, are used for specific applications like humidity calculations in natural gas.

Key Standards and Formulas:

Sonntag Equation: A widely used formula for water vapor pressure found in British Standard 1339:1.

Hyland-Wexler Formula: Another formula for water vapor pressure, often used in specific temperature ranges.

Industry-Specific Standards: For specific applications like natural gas, other standards such as ISO 18453 and IGT Research Bulletin #8 are used.

Therefore: When using a relative saturation (humidity) calculator, it's best to check the documentation or settings to see which specific formula or standard it is built upon.

🔚 Conclusion

This calculator provides reliable estimates of relative humidity, supporting QEHS compliance, HVAC design, and environmental monitoring. Ensure input values (pₐ, Pₐ) are accurate and validate with standards like ASHRAE 55, British Standard 1339:1, or field measurements for critical applications to mitigate risks like condensation, corrosion, and static electricity.
