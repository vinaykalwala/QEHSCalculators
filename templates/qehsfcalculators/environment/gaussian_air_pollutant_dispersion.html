üìñ Governing Principle

A "Gaussian Air Pollutant Dispersion Calculator" isn't based on a single "standard" but rather uses the Gaussian plume model itself, a widely adopted scientific framework for simulating how pollutants disperse in the atmosphere, assuming a normal distribution of concentration across the plume. While it was once considered a standard for routine regulatory calculations, various factors like atmospheric stability and wind conditions influence the specific formulas and parameters used within the model.

Key Aspects of the Gaussian Model

Assumptions: The model assumes pollutant concentration follows a normal (Gaussian) distribution both horizontally and vertically, spreading like a bell curve from the plume's centerline.

Parameters: It requires inputs such as:

  • Emission rate (Q): The amount of pollutant released.
  • Wind speed (u): The speed of the wind.
  • Effective stack height (H): The effective height of the emission source.
  • Atmospheric stability categories: Factors like A (extremely unstable) to F (moderately stable) that describe how turbulent the atmosphere is.

Limitations: The model has limitations, including:

  • Assumptions: Its accuracy can be affected by simplified assumptions about atmospheric conditions and terrain.
  • Calm conditions: The standard formula may not be accurate under calm wind conditions.
