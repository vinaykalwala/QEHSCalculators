📘 About This Calculator

The Latent Heat Gain Calculator computes the total heat added to a space due to human occupancy. This is critical for HVAC design, indoor air quality, and environmental safety in commercial and industrial settings.

⚙️ Parameters

  • Number of People: Total occupants in the space (1 – 1000)
  • Latent Heat Gain per Person: Heat gain per occupant in Btu/hr (range 50 – 500)

✅ Input Validations

  • ✔️ Number of People: Positive integer, min 1, max 1000 (for large buildings)
  • ✔️ Latent Heat Gain per Person: Positive, typical range 50–500 Btu/hr depending on activity level
  • ❌ Non-numeric, negative, or zero inputs are invalid
  • ⚠️ Extremely high total heat gain (>10,000 Btu/hr) will trigger a warning for potential indoor climate issues

✅ Output Validations

Based on the calculated Total Latent Heat Gain (Qlatent), the system provides an automatic status message:

  • ✅ Qlatent ≤ 3000 Btu/hr: Heat gain is within a manageable range.
  • ⚠️ 3000 < Qlatent ≤ 10,000 Btu/hr: Moderate heat gain – monitor indoor climate carefully.
  • 🔴 Qlatent > 10,000 Btu/hr:High heat gain – action required to ensure indoor comfort and safety.

📋 Governing Principle

A "Latent Heat Gain from People Calculator" relies on data and guidelines from the ASHRAE (American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers), specifically from its ASHRAE Fundamentals Handbook, which provides tables and methods for calculating heat gain from various internal sources, including people.

How it works:

Heat Partition: People in a space generate both sensible (temperature) and latent (moisture/vapor) heat. The latent heat comes from the body's perspiration and respiration.
Activity Levels: The amount of latent heat gain varies significantly based on the person's activity level.
ASHRAE Data: The calculator uses values from ASHRAE tables that provide latent heat gain per person for different activities (e.g., working in an office versus performing heavy manual work).
Calculation: To determine the total latent heat gain, you multiply the latent heat gain per person by the number of people in the space.

Key Resources:

ASHRAE Fundamentals Handbook: This handbook contains the detailed tables and methodologies for calculating these heat loads.
The Engineering ToolBox: Provides example tables of metabolic heat gain from people, which reference older ISO and ASHRAE standards and can be used for estimating loads.
