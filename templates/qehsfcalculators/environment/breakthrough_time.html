Assessment & Suggestions

📘 About

This calculator estimates how long it takes for leachate to migrate through a clay liner based on thickness, porosity, permeability and driving head. Use consistent units (ft for length and ft/yr for conductivity).

📏 Units & typical ranges

  • Thickness (d): feet (ft). Typical clay liner thickness: 1–6 ft.
  • Porosity (η): fraction (0–1). Typical clay: 0.10–0.30.
  • Hydraulic Conductivity (K): ft/yr. Clay is often very low (e.g. 1e-6 to 1e-2 ft/yr depending on compaction).
  • Hydraulic Head (h): feet (ft). Driving head depends on leachate depth and site conditions.
  • Make sure to convert units (e.g., m → ft, m/yr → ft/yr) before entering values.

✅ Input Validations

  • Thickness (d): Must be > 0 ft. Warning if < 0.1 ft (too thin) or > 50 ft (check units).
  • Porosity (η): Must be a fraction between 0 and 1. Warning if < 0.10 or > 0.30 (outside typical clay range). Values <0.01 or >0.60 are flagged as extreme.
  • Hydraulic Conductivity (K): Must be > 0 ft/yr. Warning if ≤ 1×10⁻⁹ (too small) or > 1×10⁶ (too large). Extra warnings if values suggest ft/day or ft/s were entered instead of ft/yr.
  • Hydraulic Head (h): Must be ≥ 0 ft. Warning if > 1×10⁶ ft (unit mismatch likely) or if h ≫ d.
  • Cross-checks: Denominator (d + h) must be > 0. Computed breakthrough time must be finite and positive.
  • General: All fields are required, must be finite numbers (scientific notation allowed). Units must be consistent.

🔎 Interpretation

  • Very Long (≫ 100 years): liner effectively delays breakthrough — verify inputs and long-term performance considerations.
  • Moderate (10–100 years): typical protective performance depending on regulations.
  • Short (< 10 years): potential risk — investigate compaction, K reduction measures, or additional barriers.

🛑 Limitations

  • Assumes uniform, intact clay with no cracks or preferential flow paths.
  • Does not include chemical diffusion, sorption, or biodegradation effects.
  • Site heterogeneity, freeze–thaw, desiccation or construction defects can drastically shorten actual breakthrough time.
